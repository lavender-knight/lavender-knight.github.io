The History of Art in Video Games

     There are many ways of showing off art: movies, writing, music, paintings, etc. A new medium has arose that, in my opinion, trumps them all. Throughout the past 50 years video games have developed from blocky and unidentifyable to stunning and realistic. (Not to say that pixel art games are not stunning of course). Pong released in 1972 marking itself as the first video game and at the time almost everything the technology we had could handle. At the time I don't think the developers were thinking of the birth of a medium but rather just trying to find a fun way to pass the time.

     The blocky 8-bit games were very influential to the industry, take Super Mario Bros. (1985) for example-- essentially the introduction of home consoles and the introduction of characters that people have grown to know and love and a name that everyone knows. If you were to ask a random grandma on the street to "name a video game" she would probably respond with Mario. The Nintendo Entertainment System (NES) screen space is sized at an itty-bitty 256 x 224px. The NES could show a max of 64 colors and only 25 were able to be onscreen at any given time. From an artist standpoint, this is extreme limitations that you have to work under to make anything visually interesting.

     When artists really got the hang of the limitations they were given for the start of the art medium of video games there were plenty that looked fantastic for their time. For example, Super Metroid, a cult-classic mastering the use of the limited color pallete! Same goes for Castlevania Symphony of The Night where they took advantage of the fact that their pixelated graphics were going to be shown on CRT monitors/T.V.'s.
